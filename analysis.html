8The Calculation: Where The Extra Energy Goes

METHOD: time-varying baseline + known element power (3.11 kW) Household baseline built from confirmed-clean measurement windows: • OLD regime: hours 08:00–09:00 (heater firing <2%) • NEW regime: hours 16:00–19:30 (heater explicitly OFF) • Remaining hours: circular interpolation Baseline ranges from 130W (06:00, overnight) to 450W (19:00, peak evening). For each slot: excess = reading − hourly baseline, capped at 1.555 kWh. OLD REGIME (Mar–May 2025): 16.4 kWh/day Heater: 11.0 kWh/day • Element run-time: 3.5 h/day Household: 5.4 kWh/day SUMMER (Jul–Aug 2025): 14.6 kWh/day Heater: 9.1 kWh/day • Element run-time: 2.9 h/day Household: 5.5 kWh/day NEW REGIME WINTER (Dec 2025–Jan 2026): 26.1 kWh/day Heater: 19.5 kWh/day • Element run-time: 6.3 h/day Household: 6.7 kWh/day THE GAP (new vs old): +5.4 kWh/day Heater increase: +4.7 kWh/day (86% of gap) ← TIMER REGIME CHANGE Household increase: +0.7 kWh/day (14% of gap) ← SEASONAL VARIATION WHY DOES THE SAME HEATER USE MORE ENERGY? Old regime: element enabled ~6 h/day (in cheap-rate windows) New regime: element enabled ~20 h/day (24/7 minus 16–20) Old regime: tank held at thermostat setpoint only during windows. Between windows, tank cooled freely. No energy consumed. Average tank temperature across the day: well BELOW setpoint. Lower avg temp → lower heat loss rate → less energy needed. New regime: tank held at FULL setpoint ~20 hours/day. Every temperature dip immediately corrected. Average tank temperature across the day: AT or NEAR setpoint. Higher avg temp → higher heat loss rate → more energy needed. The old enforced off-periods acted as free temperature setback. The new regime eliminates that setback = more standing losses replenished. ELEMENT: 3.11 kW (measured). Identical in both regimes. It's not a different element. Not a changed thermostat. Not more hot water usage. It's purely more run-time: 3.5 h/day → 6.3 h/day.
